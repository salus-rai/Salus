Introduction

AI security is about protecting AI systems from malicious attacks and ensuring the safety and reliability of AI models and data. It involves measures to identify vulnerabilities, mitigate risks, manipulation, and exploitation of the AI system and safeguard the functionality and trustworthiness of AI systems.

Key Concepts in AI Security

  • Robustness: Ensuring that AI models are resistant to adversarial attacks and can maintain accurate performance even when faced with manipulated or misleading inputs.

  • Confidentiality: Protecting the privacy and confidentiality of AI models and sensitive data used in training and inference processes.

  • Integrity: Ensuring the integrity of AI models and data by preventing unauthorized access, manipulation, or tampering.

  • Availability: Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information.

Tools for AI Security

  • Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART): An open-source Python library that provides tools for defending and evaluating machine learning models against adversarial attacks.

  • Counterfit : An open-source automation tool for security testing AI systems. It helps organizations assess the robustness and reliability of their AI algorithms.

  • AI Shield: A licensed product offered by Bosch, designed to provide real-time defense and protection for AI systems, especially against attacks not supported by open-source tools.

  • Infosys Responsible AI Toolkit: An initiative by Infosys that focuses on promoting responsible and ethical AI practices. The toolkit helps organizations develop and deploy AI systems with fairness, transparency, ethics, and accountability.

These tools offer functionalities such as testing AI systems for vulnerabilities, evaluating robustness against attacks and address failures in AI systems.

Key Points on AI Security Policy

1. Data Privacy and Compliance

  • Ensure compliance with data protection regulations like GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA when handling personal or sensitive data.

  • Implement anonymization and encryption techniques to protect sensitive data used for training or inference in AI systems.

  • Data retention and access controls should be defined, ensuring that only authorized personnel can access and manage the data.

2. Model Security and Access Control

  • Protect models from unauthorized access, theft, or manipulation. Implement strict authentication and authorization mechanisms.

  • Access control policies should restrict who can modify or fine-tune AI models to prevent malicious tampering or adversarial exploitation.

  • Encryption of model weights and parameters can help prevent reverse engineering of proprietary models.

3. Adversarial Attack Prevention

  • Develop defenses against adversarial attacks that manipulate model predictions or outputs by introducing perturbations into inputs.

  • Adversarial training should be considered as part of the model development process to improve robustness against such attacks.

  • Regular testing and monitoring should be conducted to detect any signs of vulnerabilities in the system.

4. Content Moderation and Ethical Safeguards

  • Implement content moderation tools to prevent the AI from generating harmful or malicious outputs (e.g., hate speech, misinformation, or deepfakes).

  • Enforce ethical guidelines to ensure AI-generated content is aligned with societal norms and ethical standards.

  • Bias mitigation measures should be in place to prevent the AI from perpetuating harmful biases based on race, gender, or other attributes.

5. Transparency and Accountability

  • Ensure model explainability and transparency so that users can understand how decisions are made by AI systems, especially in high-risk or high-stakes areas.

  • Maintain audit trails of model actions and data interactions to track decision-making and hold entities accountable for AI-generated results.

  • Establish clear responsibility for any errors or misuse of AI, including liability frameworks for developers, users, and organizations deploying the models.

6. Incident Response and Recovery

  • Develop a robust incident response plan that includes procedures for detecting, reporting, and mitigating security breaches or adversarial attacks on AI systems.

  • Monitor system behavior continuously to detect anomalies or signs of security threats, and have a recovery plan in place for restoring the AI model to a secure state if compromised.

7. AI Ethics and Social Impact

  • Promote responsible AI usage by setting guidelines that prevent AI from being used for malicious purposes, such as spreading misinformation, generating harmful content, or violating user rights.

  • Consider the social impact of AI deployment, ensuring it does not disproportionately harm vulnerable groups or communities.

  • Regular audits should be conducted to assess whether the AI systems are meeting ethical standards and guidelines.

8. Collaboration and Best Practices

  • Collaborate with industry standards organizations to ensure alignment with global AI security frameworks and best practices.

  • Encourage transparency and sharing of threat intelligence within the AI development and security communities to improve collective defense against new threats.

9. Continuous Monitoring and Updates

  • Monitor the performance and behavior of AI models continuously in real-world applications to ensure ongoing security and identify potential risks.

  • Regularly update models and systems to patch vulnerabilities, incorporate new security measures, and improve defense mechanisms against evolving threats.

10. Training and Awareness

  • Train employees and stakeholders on AI security best practices, ethical guidelines, and how to handle sensitive data.

  • Raise awareness about the potential risks associated with generative AI and other AI systems to ensure informed decision-making and responsible use.

Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART) library

ART is a Python library for Machine Learning Security. ART is hosted by the Linux Foundation AI & Data Foundation (LF AI & Data). ART provides tools that enable developers and researchers to defend and evaluate Machine Learning models and applications against the adversarial threats of Evasion, Poisoning, Extraction and Inference. ART supports all popular machine learning frameworks (TensorFlow, Keras, PyTorch, MXNet, scikit-learn, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, GPy, etc), all data types (images, tables, audio, video, etc.) and machine learning tasks (classification, object detection, speech recognition, generation, certification, etc.).

Bergeron: Combating Adversarial Attacks through a Conscience-Based Alignment Framework

Source : https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Bergeron%3A-Combating-Adversarial-Attacks-through-a-Pisano-Ly/ecdfc556828dc59d8a0009016fcbe97a06cf7e23

Testing Report : [Excel] [Excel]

 

Research into AI alignment has grown considerably since the recent introduction of increasingly capable Large Language Models (LLMs). Unfortunately, modern methods of alignment still fail to fully prevent harmful responses when models are deliberately attacked. These attacks can trick seemingly aligned models into giving manufacturing instructions for dangerous materials, inciting violence, or recommending other immoral acts. To help mitigate this issue, we introduce Bergeron: a framework designed to improve the robustness of LLMs against attacks without any additional parameter fine-tuning. Bergeron is organized into two tiers: with a secondary LLM emulating the conscience of a protected, primary LLM. This framework better safeguards the primary model against incoming attacks while monitoring its output for any harmful content.

 

Framework Components:

          The Bergeron framework is composed of two main components: the primary model and the secondary model.

Primary model: Here, the primary model can be any LLM. It is unchanged with no special fine-tuning. It receives prompts from the user and modified prompts from the secondary model if needed.

Secondary model: The secondary model serves as the primary model's conscience. It judges incoming prompts as safe or unsafe as well as the outgoing responses from the primary model. The goal is to:

  1. Prevent unsafe prompts from reaching the primary model.

  2. Prevent unsafe responses from reaching the end user if step 1 fails.

 

                        As Bergeron is using two models there is computational cost that comes along with this. So, our primary goal is to modify this framework for using only one LLM.

The dependency of two LLMs is now minimized by using only one LLM.

 

Functionality:        

  • The user provided input prompt will be passed to a template and this will be generating a critique prompt i.e., If harmful or dangerous contents are detected, a suggestion will be generated.

  • This Critique prompt is then passed to the LLM for getting the response where this response will be having a label:

    • ADVERSARIAL - For any Adversarial prompts (Jailbreak prompts, prompt injection prompts)

    • NON-ADVERSARIAL - For Safer prompts

  • The Critique prompt is then checked for the labels (ADVERSARIAL/ NON-ADVERSARIAL) then based on the result we are concluding this as either ADVERSARIAL/ NON-ADVERSARIAL

 

 Inferencing:

We are testing this Bergeron for two large language models:

  • Gpt-35-turbo

  • Gpt_4_Turbo_Preview

SmoothLLM: Defensive Technique Against Jailbreaking Attacks

Source : [2310.03684] SmoothLLM: Defending Large Language Models Against Jailbreaking Attacks (arxiv.org)

 

Despite efforts to align large language models (LLMs) with human values, widely-used LLMs such as GPT, Llama, Claude, and PaLM are susceptible to attacks, wherein an adversary fools a targeted LLM into generating objectionable content. To address this vulnerability, we propose SmoothLLM, the first algorithm designed to mitigate attacks on LLMs. Based on our finding that adversarially generated prompts are brittle to character-level changes, our defense first randomly perturbs multiple copies of a given input prompt, and then aggregates the corresponding predictions to detect adversarial inputs. SmoothLLM reduces the attack success rate on numerous popular LLMs to below one percentage point, avoids unnecessary conservatism, and admits provable guarantees on attack mitigation. Moreover, our defense uses exponentially fewer queries than existing attacks and is compatible with any LLM.

Functionality:

SmoothLLM mainly contains two steps:

  • Perturbation Step

  • Aggregation Step

Perturbation Step:

The first ingredient in our approach is to randomly perturb prompts passed as input to the LLM.  Consider q is the percentage of perturbed characters, A is the character that is to be perturbed, P is the input prompt. Now consider these three perturbations:

  • Insert: Randomly sample q% of the characters in P, and after each of these characters, insert a new character sampled uniformly from A.

  • Swap: Randomly sample q% of the characters in P, and then swap the characters at those locations by sampling new characters uniformly from A.

  • Patch: Randomly sample d consecutive characters in P, where d equals q% of the characters in P, and then replace these characters with new characters sampled uniformly from A.

Aggregation Step:
The second key ingredient is as follows: Rather than passing a single perturbed prompt through the LLM, we obtain a collection of perturbed prompts, and we then aggregate the predictions corresponding to this collection.

 

 

  • If the smoothLLM score is greater than or equal to threshold then the result is Failed which means the provided input prompt has some malicious content in it.

  • If the smoothLLM score is less than threshold then the result is Passed which means the provided prompt is a safe prompt.

We have utilized the Swap function for our experiments and validated on multiple prompts and obtained the expected results.

Inferencing:

We tested SmoothLLM for two large language models.

  • Gpt-35-turbo

  • Gpt_4_Turbo_Preview

DoS (Deniel of Service)

Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks can overwhelm a server with a large volume of requests, making it unavailable to legitimate users.

Rate Limiting

Rate limiting is a technique to mitigate DoS attacks by restricting the number of requests a user can send within a specific timeframe. By regulating request traffic, rate limiting prevents your server from being overloaded, ensuring optimal performance of LLM.

 

For FastAPI application, we can leverage FastAPI-Limiter and Redis in order to create a robust rate limiting system :-

  • FastAPI-Limiter is a popular library for integrating rate limiting functionality into FastAPI applications. It offers a flexible and efficient way to control the request rate for your API endpoints. We can customize the aspects of rate limiting in FastAPI-Limiter:

    • Limits per User: Define the maximum number of requests allowed per user within the specified time window.

    • Time Windows: Set the duration for the rate limit window (e.g., per minute, per hour).

  • Redis is a powerful in-memory data store that acts as the backend for storing rate limiting information. You'll need to configure a Redis server and establish a connection from your FastAPI application.

Also when using the frameworks like Langchain and LLamaIndex, sometimes LLM can do some repetitions of a task by a single prompt. These repetitions, although not infinite, can be undesirable. To address this, these frameworks offer parameters to control the LLM's behavior like:

  • Maximum Number of Iterations: You can set a limit on the number of times the LLM attempts to complete a task based on a single prompt. This helps prevent the LLM from getting stuck in a loop and generating redundant outputs.

  • Time Limit: Define a maximum time allowed for the LLM to complete a task. This ensures the LLM doesn't spend excessive time on a single prompt, potentially leading to repetitive outputs.
